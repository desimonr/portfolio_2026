Objective

At the end of 2020, Northwestern Medicine (NM) launched a proprietary NLP-powered chatbot on NM.org to address overwhelming patient demand for self-service options. Built on Microsoft Azure Language Services, the chatbot used natural language processing to understand patient intent and route users to the right information — from finding a doctor to paying a bill.

My role was to ensure that the chatbot's human-written responses and the AI system's configured personality worked together as a seamless, consistent experience. I worked with stakeholders across the health system to develop formal voice guidelines that would govern both the copy written by content teams and the tone parameters configured in the NLP model itself.

The Design Challenge

Designing voice for a conversational AI system is more complex than defining a brand voice for static content. The chatbot's responses emerge from two interacting layers: human-written copy authored by content writers, and personality parameters configured directly in the NLP model. A misalignment between these two layers would produce an inconsistent, untrustworthy user experience — especially problematic in healthcare, where patients are often stressed or confused.

The challenge was to define a voice precise enough to guide both layers simultaneously: specific enough that copywriters could apply it consistently, and translatable enough that it could inform the model's tone configuration settings.

What is Voice?

Before jumping into the research, I knew that defining the voice would involve understanding and categorizing emotions — a process with inherent ambiguity and subjectivity. While there are many frameworks on the topic, Nielsen Norman Group (NNG) took the most data-focused approach, defining tone and voice across four measurable dimensions. I adapted this scale as the foundation for my research.

Competitive Analysis

I wanted to identify trends in healthcare virtual assistants and establish a benchmark for where NM sat in the landscape. I performed a competitive analysis by plotting each competitor's chatbot along the NNG dimensions using standardized five-point scales. Several competitors had chatbots and one had an Alexa skill, which I charted alongside NM's existing brand guidelines.

Healthcare competitors generally aligned with each other — academic, measured, and professional. However, when I added Google Assistant and Siri to the analysis to capture broader conversational AI trends, a clear opportunity emerged: most healthcare chatbots were lagging significantly behind general-purpose voice assistants in warmth and friendliness. This presented an opportunity for NM to differentiate.

Stakeholder Survey

The chatbot's implementation touched teams across the health system — brand, clinical, and operational. I designed a structured survey to draw on their collective expertise in patient interactions and customer service, using the NNG dimensions as the survey framework so responses would be directly applicable to both the guidelines and the model configuration.

The results were surprising: stakeholders largely supported moving the voice toward friendliness, beyond what the existing brand guidelines prescribed. "Friendly" tied with "patients" as the most common descriptor when stakeholders were asked to characterize the ideal virtual assistant voice.

Across all four dimensions, a majority of stakeholders agreed to shift one point toward the warmer, friendlier end of the scale — represented by the blue dot in the charts below.

Guidelines

Using the research and survey results, I developed formal voice guidelines covering both the written copy and the NLP model's tone parameters. The guidelines were structured so that content writers could apply them independently, while also providing the development team with a clear rationale for how to configure the model's personality settings to match.

After a round of stakeholder presentations and revisions, the guidelines were approved across all teams — brand, clinical, and operations — and distributed as the authoritative standard for all NM virtual assistant experiences.

Expanding to Voice: The Alexa Opportunity

During the project, I learned that a parallel initiative was underway to deploy Amazon Alexa devices in hospital rooms — allowing patients to ask simple questions and get answers without requiring a nurse visit. This surfaced a new design challenge: how do the same voice guidelines translate from a screen-based chat interface to a voice-only ambient device?

I expanded my research scope to include voice assistant design principles specific to the clinical bedside context. Patients interacting with a hospital room device face a distinct set of constraints — they may be post-procedure, on medication, in pain, or have limited mobility. Voice-only interaction removes all visual feedback, making clarity, brevity, and appropriate tone even more critical. I documented these considerations as an addendum to the core guidelines, giving the Alexa team a foundation to build on.

Outcome

The chatbot launched initially under pressure from a surge in COVID-19 inquiries, with responses copied directly from existing NM.org content. Over time, content writers systematically rewrote responses to align with the voice guidelines — and the impact was measurable. The system achieved a 95% intent recognition rate, meaning the NLP model successfully understood and routed 95 out of every 100 patient questions. By April 2021, the chatbot was handling over 3,800 conversations per month — more than double the volume from its first months — with patient interaction counts continuing to climb through the following year.

The voice guidelines remain the authoritative standard for all NM virtual assistant experiences, covering both the chatbot and future voice-based implementations.
